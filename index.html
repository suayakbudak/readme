Hello, You From HR

Yes, you.
The final boss. The gatekeeper. The nightmare of candidates. The one who decides if I eat or starve.

The fact that you actually read my CV and made it this far? Impressive. Rare, even. Most CVs end up in the "maybe later" folder — which is code for "never."
So genuinely — thank you.

I'm Suay.
27 years old. Software Engineer. Cybersecurity Engineer.

  • 5 years freelance software development
  • 1 year 5 months in IT security & auditing
  • 6+ years total experience

Currently unemployed.

Now you're probably wondering: How? What went wrong? Is there a red flag I'm hiding?

Let's rewind.

PART 01 — The Story Behind the CV

While I was studying Software Engineering (English) at Bahçeşehir University, COVID hit.

Internships froze. Hiring stopped. The world paused. Because why would a pandemic care about graduation plans?

I graduated with five months of experience and spectacularly bad timing.

Because of health risks and companies insisting on on-site work, my internship — along with other interns — was terminated.

Bad luck?
Probably.

Wrong era to graduate?
Definitely.

PART 02 — The Five-Month Detour

September 2022. I joined a big-name firm. Five months.

The managers? Professional gossip enthusiasts. The kind who discuss who wore what, who said what, and who probably thought what — all before their first coffee.

I dealt with anxiety. A lot. "I don't belong here" became my internal mantra. I repeated it in the elevator, at my desk, in the bathroom mirror.

It wasn't impostor syndrome. It was clarity.

Some places feel like a glove. That place felt like someone else's skin.

So I left. No drama. No burning bridges. Just a quiet exit before the gossip machine could add me to its rotation.

PART 03 — The Gap Nobody Likes

Yes. Three years.

No shortcuts. No safety net. Just depression, financial pressure, and responsibility.

While others built careers through stable paths, I was handling survival mode. My mother underwent emergency stomach surgery. Her wound reopened. For six months, I managed open wound care at home — while actively job hunting and dealing with my own necessary surgeries.

There's no checkbox for that on a CV.

What those three years taught me:

  • Discipline under pressure
  • Emotional regulation in crisis
  • Technical focus despite chaos
  • Responsibility without excuses

I can write code.
I can manage systems.
Apparently, I can also manage surgical wound care.

But here's the important part:

Resilient does not mean endlessly available.
Capable does not mean 24/7 online.

I believe in structured working hours (9–6 / 8–5).
Sustainable productivity > burnout culture.

The gap exists.
It also built strength most roles never test for.

PART 04 — The Builder Phase

While the market was unstable and everyone was panicking, I kept building. Panic is optional. Progress isn't.

My first client was a kind woman from Poland. I'll always be grateful — for the first time, I was able to meaningfully support my family.

Around 15 clients — most came back. Repeat customers.

No ads. No influencer persona. No "LinkedIn tech prophet" energy. No "I wake at 4 AM and cold plunge" flex.

I didn't want attention. I wanted skill.

So I worked quietly. Delivered consistently. Improved both technically and socially.

Then I joined a startup.

Irregular hours. Nights. Weekends. No insurance. The startup dream package — minus the dream.

We delivered a large-scale website for the Ministry of Energy and Natural Resources in one week.

Then came the twist:

"You'll start getting paid after three months."

Bold strategy.

Lesson learned.

After that, I joined a multinational enterprise operating in energy, infrastructure and industrial technology.

Finally — structure.

For one year, I worked in cybersecurity risk management within a globally regulated corporate environment.

This wasn’t theoretical security. It was enterprise-level risk governance.

  • Cybersecurity risk assessments & control evaluations
  • IT General Controls (ITGC) testing
  • Access management & segregation of duties reviews
  • Risk heatmaps, mitigation tracking & reporting
  • Alignment with corporate security policies & global compliance standards
  • Stakeholder communication across technical and business units

Enterprise systems. Formal risk registers. Documented accountability.

No guessing. No chaos. Measured impact.

Corporate cybersecurity taught me one thing clearly:

Security isn’t fear. It’s governance.

One year later, restructuring affected part-time roles — including mine.

And here we are. Still standing. Still typing. Still sarcastic.
